EMP Gun

I began my electronics career by building a EMP gun after watching a YouTube video about it. The device works by exploiting a fundamental property of physics called induction. As an electron moves (electrical current), it makes a magenetic field, and the same logic applies in the opposite: as a magnetic field changes, it induces a current in a nearby conductor.
By using a bug zapper and a spark gap to create fast pulses, my EMP gun generates a rapidly changing magnetic field. This field can induce currents in nearby electronics, which can fry them.

June 16th 2022

The final product looks messy, but I wanted to give it a 'DIY' feel. It works by using a battery (thin blue box on the bottom front) to power the circuit from a bug zapper (main circuit board), which has a small spark gap connected to an air inductor (wire coil).
I took some measurements on my oscilioscope, and saw a minimum of -1.04 volts which is enough to destroy some components due to the unprotected reverse polarity.

Smart Switch

In my house, I am forced to walk to every light switch in order to toggle it. For my second electrical project I decided to design a remote controlled switch that could act as a hand pressing something. In order to plan for this project, I was forced to learn about schematics and to memorize the symbols for each component. I put what I had learned to the test with this project by drawing my first schematic. While it was very simple, and there are a thousand different things that I would do now differently, it actually worked.

December 21, 2022

I had no concept of infrared sensors or subGHz communications, so I stripped apart a remote controlled light and detected if it was on with a photosensitive diode. I then used an arduino to control a small servo motor which could press something it was taped to. I also made an attempt to block external frequencies with a polarized 100 microfarad capacitor (which didn't really do anything).
Even with all of its flaws, I still believe this smart switch project was a turning point where I used my limited knowledge to solve problems in creative ways.

December 29, 2022

Online shocker

To put my coding and electronics skills to the test, I decided to build a website that could trigger a shocking device from anywhere in the world. I used a Raspberry Pi to host the webserver, and to interface with the shocking device. Later, I replaced the LED seen in the photo with a servo motor that would press the button to trigger the shock device. I tested the website with my friend in California, and he was able to trigger different things with a latency of less than 400ms.
